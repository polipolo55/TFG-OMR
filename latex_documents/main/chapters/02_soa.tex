\chapter{State of the Art}
\label{ch:soa}

Following the two published surveys of the
field~\cite{calvoZaragozaUnderstandingOMR2020,shatriOPTICALMUSICRECOGNITION},
this chapter groups prior OMR work into three broadly distinguishable
generations: the heuristic modular pipelines that defined the field from
the 1990s through the early 2010s; the convolutional--recurrent sequence
models trained with Connectionist Temporal Classification (CRNN--CTC) that
arose with deep learning; and the most recent end-to-end transformer
architectures, which scale to polyphonic and full-page input at the cost of
substantially larger labelled corpora.
This chapter surveys those paradigms, reviews the principal datasets and
symbolic output representations used in the literature, and positions this
thesis with respect to recent work that targets jazz lead sheets specifically.

\section{The Classical OMR Pipeline}
\label{sec:soa-classical}

Until the mid-2010s, almost every OMR system was a modular pipeline of four
sequential stages: image preprocessing (binarisation, deskewing); staff-line
detection and removal; segmentation and classification of the musical
primitives left behind once the staff lines have been erased; and semantic
reconstruction, in which the recognised primitives are assembled into a
structured score~\cite{calvoZaragozaUnderstandingOMR2020}.
\Cref{fig:soa-arch-bainbridge,fig:soa-arch-rebelo} show two
influential illustrations of this decomposition, both predating the
deep-learning shift discussed in \Cref{sec:soa-crnn}.

The second stage was historically the most studied because every subsequent
step depended on it.
\textcite{dalitzComparativeStudyStaff2008} compared the principal
families of staff-removal algorithms and contributed a skeletonisation-based
method; the \term{CVC-MUSCIMA} corpus~\cite{fornesCVCMUSCIMAGroundTruth2012}
was constructed expressly to benchmark this stage.

\term{Audiveris}~\cite{audiverisAudiveris} is the most widely used
open-source modular OMR engine.
Its processing chain combines morphological operations for staff and
beam handling, template matching for fixed-shape symbols, a small
convolutional classifier for ambiguous glyphs, and external OCR for
lyrics, exporting MusicXML~4.
The architecture is representative of the whole family.
Established commercial systems such as SmartScore, PhotoScore, and
SharpEye follow the same general decomposition but are
proprietary~\cite{calvoZaragozaUnderstandingOMR2020}.

Two structural weaknesses limit the modular approach for the input class
targeted in this thesis.
Errors accumulate irreversibly across stages: a single missed staff
line corrupts every downstream pitch assignment for that staff, and a
mis-segmented chord symbol cannot be recovered by modules that have already
committed to a different reading.
The visual heuristics driving each module are also tuned for classical
engraving styles; the rounded noteheads, loosely-spaced beams, and
hand-lettered chord symbols of \textit{The Real Book} fall
outside the operating envelope those pipelines assume.
Both weaknesses point toward end-to-end, learning-based recognition.

\section{Deep Learning Paradigm: CRNN with CTC}
\label{sec:soa-crnn}

The deep-learning era of OMR begins with
\textcite{calvoZaragozaEndToEndNeural2018}, who in 2018 applied to
single-staff music the CRNN--CTC architecture that had recently become
standard for scene-text and handwritten-text recognition.
The model treats a staff image as a sequence of vertical strips, extracts
local visual features through a convolutional backbone, contextualises them
with a bidirectional recurrent network, and decodes the resulting per-frame
distribution into a token sequence using the CTC
alignment~\cite{gravesConnectionistTemporal2006}, removing the need for
per-symbol segmentation~\cite{shiEndToEndTrainable2017}.

Alongside the architecture, Calvo-Zaragoza and Rizo released the
\term{PrIMuS} corpus~\cite{PrIMuSDataset,calvoZaragozaEndToEndNeural2018}: 87\,678 real-music monophonic
incipits sourced from the R\'epertoire International des Sources
Musicales (RISM) and distributed in five
representations, including two purpose-built token formats.
The \term{agnostic} encoding lists visual primitives without musical
interpretation; the \term{semantic} encoding lists musical events such as
\code{note-C4\_eighth} or \code{keySignature-DM} directly.
The CRNN--CTC architecture trained on this corpus achieves a Symbol Error
Rate (SER) of \SI{1.0}{\percent} on the agnostic encoding and \SI{0.8}{\percent}
on the semantic encoding, with sequence-level error rates of
\SI{17.9}{\percent} and \SI{12.5}{\percent} respectively
(\textcite{calvoZaragozaEndToEndNeural2018}, Sec.~5.2).
This established the reference baseline that subsequent monophonic OMR
work, including Camera-PrIMuS and the present thesis, builds upon.
A follow-up release, Camera-PrIMuS~\cite{calvoZaragozaCameraPrimus2018},
augments the same incipits with synthetic photographic distortion and
shows that the same network retains useful accuracy on
phone-captured real-world inputs.

to a music staff.

\section{End-to-End Transformer Architectures}
\label{sec:soa-transformer}

From 2024 onward several leading OMR systems have adopted attentional
encoder--decoder transformers for tasks that exceed the scope of monophonic
single-staff recognition.
The \term{Sheet Music Transformer}
(SMT)~\cite{riosVilaSheetMusicTransformer2024} was the first fully
transformer-based image-to-sequence OMR system to address polyphonic
transcription end-to-end: a vision-transformer encoder produces a flattened
patch sequence which a stacked decoder autoregressively maps to the
linearised target representation, dispensing with CTC entirely.
\term{SMT++}~\cite{riosVilaSMTPlusPlus2024} extends the approach to
full-page pianoform input and, at its 2024 release, defined the state of
the art on that task.
\term{LEGATO}~\cite{yangLEGATO2025}, released in mid-2025, takes the
trend further with a vision-LLM backbone, an ABC-notation decoder, and over
214\,000 typeset pages of pretraining, reporting on its most challenging
evaluation set absolute error reductions of up to \SI{68}{\percent} on the
tree-edit metric TEDn and \SI{47.6}{\percent} on OMR-NED over the previous
state of the art.

\Cref{fig:soa-transformer-pipeline} shows the generic encoder--decoder
structure shared by these systems.

\input{figures/fig_transformer_pipeline}

These architectures, however, demand resources that exceed the budget of
a Bachelor's thesis.
For a strictly monophonic target domain, where the CRNN--CTC baseline
already attains sub-percent SER and the headroom for further gain lies in
domain robustness rather than model capacity, CRNN--CTC remains the more
economical choice.
The quantitative justification is in \Cref{ch:design}; the
project-management argument is in \Cref{sec:mgmt-justification}.

The data strategy of this thesis follows from a gap in this table.
To the author's knowledge none of these corpora captures the
LilyJAZZ-style engraving of \textit{The Real Book}, and none labels chord
symbols as first-class objects. The only directly comparable corpus emerged
contemporaneously with this work~\cite{martinezSevillaJazzLeadSheets2025}
and, at 293 annotated sheets, is far smaller than the tens of
thousands of samples those CRNN--CTC models require, and so cannot train
one from scratch. Every recent end-to-end
system (SMT, SMT++, OLiMPiC, LEGATO, and Camera-PrIMuS itself) addresses an
equivalent scarcity by synthetic pretraining followed by fine-tuning on a
small annotated target set.

\section{Symbolic Output Representations}
\label{sec:soa-representations}

The output of an OMR system is a symbolic description of the score; the
choice of representation shapes both the modelling architecture and the
achievable error rate.
\Cref{tab:omr-formats} summarises the principal options.
\textbf{MusicXML}~\cite{goodMusicXML2001} is the de facto interchange
standard between notation editors and preserves polyphony, layout, lyrics,
and chord symbols losslessly, but its verbosity inflates sequence length
and vocabulary size when fed to a sequence model.
\textbf{MEI} (Music Encoding Initiative)~\cite{rolandMEI2002} is even more
expressive but inherits the same penalty.
\textbf{Humdrum} \code{**kern}~\cite{huronHumdrum2002} and
\textbf{ABC notation}~\cite{walshawABC2011} are far more compact and are
used as targets of recent
transformer-based work~\cite{riosVilaSheetMusicTransformer2024,yangLEGATO2025},
at the cost of reduced expressiveness for complex multi-voice layout.
The PrIMuS \textbf{agnostic} and \textbf{semantic} formats are compact and
CTC-friendly but interoperate only with the PrIMuS ecosystem.

\textbf{LMX} (Linearised MusicXML), introduced by
\textcite{mayerPracticalEndToEnd2024} and maintained as an open
specification~\cite{OMRResearchLmx2025}, depth-first-traverses the
MusicXML tree and emits one whitespace-separated token per leaf event;
its grammar, encoding choices, and vocabulary are described in full in
\Cref{sec:lmx-format}.

\section{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:soa-metrics}

\textbf{Symbol Error Rate} (SER) is the de facto evaluation metric for
sequence-based OMR.
It is defined as the Levenshtein edit distance between the predicted and
the reference token sequences, normalised by the reference
length~\cite{levenshteinBinaryCodes1966}, mirroring the Word Error Rate of automatic
speech recognition.
Its principal weaknesses, flagged by both
\textcite{shatriOPTICALMUSICRECOGNITION} and
\textcite{hajicOMRMetricsEvaluation2019}, are that all token-level edits
count equally regardless of musical significance and that SER values
depend on the chosen tokenisation, so they are not directly comparable
across encodings.

Two recent metrics address these shortcomings in part.
\textbf{TEDn} (normalised tree edit distance on the parsed MusicXML tree)
weights structural errors appropriately; it was introduced by
\textcite{mayerPracticalEndToEnd2024} and is the primary metric in
OLiMPiC and SMT++.
\textbf{OMR-NED}~\cite{martinezSevillaOMRNED2025} extends SER with
per-element categories so that error distribution, not only error
rate, is legible from the metric itself.
This thesis reports SER as the primary metric for backward comparability
and complements it with a category-level error breakdown in
\Cref{ch:results}.

\section{OMR for Jazz Lead Sheets}
\label{sec:soa-jazz}

Despite the cultural importance of jazz fake-books, OMR of jazz lead sheets
received almost no dedicated attention in the published literature before
2025.
Both major surveys~\cite{calvoZaragozaUnderstandingOMR2020,shatriOPTICALMUSICRECOGNITION}
list chord-symbol handling among the gaps in the field, and, as of 2026
and to the author's knowledge, no general-purpose OMR engine treats chord
symbols as a first-class output class.

An early dedicated work is an undergraduate honours thesis at
UCF~\cite{reedHonorsLeadSheets2018}, which built a non-deep modular
pipeline for chord-symbol recognition on printed and handwritten lead
sheets and used the recovered chord progressions to attempt
formal-structure reconstruction.
The closest published precedent is
\textcite{martinezSevillaJazzLeadSheets2025},
presented at ISMIR 2025 and roughly contemporaneous with the final stages
of this thesis.
That work introduces a 293-sheet dataset of \textit{handwritten} jazz lead
sheets with \code{**kern} and MusicXML ground truth, and trains a
transformer-decoder model with symbolic-aware tokenisation, pretrained on
synthetic data and fine-tuned on the handwritten target set.

The present work differs in input domain (printed and scanned Real Book
pages versus fully handwritten sheets), in architecture (CRNN--CTC versus
transformer decoder), in chord handling (a two-stream design with
independent staff and chord networks merged in post-processing versus a
unified single-stream model), and in output format
(LMX versus \code{**kern}).
The two systems are thus complementary points in the design space that
future work can compare against and build on.
Together they are the earliest deep-learning efforts focused
specifically on jazz lead-sheet OMR; the design choices in \Cref{ch:design} follow
from this survey.
