revisited below.

\textbf{deferred to future work}, with a one-sentence justification and
a pointer to the evidence.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{O1 — State-of-the-art survey and architectural
          justification.}
          Met.
          \Cref{ch:soa} surveys the three principal families of OMR
          systems and \Cref{sec:design-pipeline-alts,sec:design-model-alts,sec:design-twostream-alts}
          adjudicate the chosen CRNN--CTC pipeline against the alternatives.
    \item \textbf{O2 — Synthetic dataset from PrIMuS in LilyJAZZ style.}
          Met.
          The render-convert-header-template-augment-vocab pipeline of
          \Cref{sec:impl-data} produces a paired

    \item \textbf{O3 — Scan-simulation augmentation pipeline.}
          Met.
          The \texttt{albumentations}-based pipeline calibrated against
          real Real Book scans is described in \Cref{sec:design-data}
          and its concrete parameter set is in \Cref{tab:aug}
          (\Cref{app:hyperparams}); its effectiveness is quantified by
          the small clean-to-scanned gap reported below.
    \item \textbf{O4 — CRNN--CTC model design, training, and SER

          Met.
          The model is implemented in \Cref{sec:impl-model}, trained
          with the protocol of \Cref{sec:design-training}, and
          evaluated against the acceptance criteria of
          \Cref{sec:design-domain}; the headline numbers reach
          \SI{0.28}{\percent} aggregate SER on the clean test split and
          \SI{0.23}{\percent} on the scanned split

    \item \textbf{O5 — Quantitative error analysis.}
          Met.
          The per-category breakdown of \Cref{sec:res-errors}

    \item \textbf{O6 — Inference pipeline with chord OCR and JSON

          The five-stage pipeline of \Cref{sec:design-pipeline} is
          realised in code at \Cref{sec:impl-inference}, exposed as a

          The end-to-end measurement on hand-annotated Real Book pages

\section{Key Quantitative Results}
\label{sec:conc-results}

The headline figures from \Cref{ch:results} are restated here for
reference. All numbers refer to the epoch-59 checkpoint at
\code{models/latest/best\_model.pt}, evaluated on 4\,608 held-out
samples per split with greedy CTC decoding.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Aggregate SER:} \SI{0.28}{\percent} (clean) and
          \SI{0.23}{\percent} (scanned).
          The two splits differ by \num{0.05} percentage points,
          well inside the one-percentage-point margin set as the
          augmentation acceptance criterion in
          \Cref{sec:design-domain}.
    \item \textbf{Melodic SER:} \SI{0.02}{\percent} (clean) and
          \SI{0.03}{\percent} (scanned), measured after stripping
          \code{measure} and tie tokens; the model gets the actual
          pitch, duration, and accidental content right on more than
          \SI{99}{\percent} of tokens.
    \item \textbf{Perfect-transcription rate:} \SI{92.1}{\percent}
          (clean) and \SI{94.1}{\percent} (scanned); more than nine
          in ten input staves come out token-identical to the ground
          truth (\Cref{sec:res-ser}).
    \item \textbf{Error composition:} approximately \SI{73}{\percent}
          of edits are \code{measure} tokens and \SI{16}{\percent} are
          ties; together these two structural classes account for
          about \SI{89}{\percent} of the residual SER, while
          per-category pitch, octave, and duration error rates each
          remain below \SI{0.05}{\percent} (\Cref{sec:res-errors}).
    \item \textbf{Beam-vs-greedy decoding:} beam search at width 5
          reduces the edit count by 8 over \num{285958} tokens
          ($\approx\num{0.003}$ pp of SER) at $\approx 8.4\times$ the
          decode cost; greedy decoding is therefore the deployed default
          (\Cref{sec:impl-inference}).
\end{itemize}

The four acceptance criteria from \Cref{sec:design-domain} are met
without exception on the synthetic test split.
The principal open question is the model's behaviour on genuine,
unaugmented Real Book pages, which is treated in
\Cref{sec:res-domaingap} and revisited as the leading future-work item.

\section{Critical Assessment and Limitations}
\label{sec:conc-limitations}

The system meets its declared targets, but the breadth of those targets
is itself a deliberate restriction.
The limitations below follow from the design decisions of
\Cref{ch:design}: some were contracted out of scope from the start,
others surfaced during implementation.

\paragraph{Scope restrictions, by design.}
The target domain is monophonic treble-clef lead sheets with the
eight common time signatures of jazz practice; the features placed
out of scope are listed in the domain contract of
\Cref{tab:domain-contract}.
None of them appear with non-negligible frequency in the Real Book,
and extending the LMX vocabulary, the grammar fixer, and the training
distribution to cover any one of them would require coordinated
changes across the entire stack
(\Cref{sec:design-data,sec:lmx-format,sec:design-grammar}).
Each is a candidate for future work.

\paragraph{Synthetic-to-real domain gap.}
The model is trained entirely on PrIMuS samples re-rendered in
LilyJAZZ and augmented with a calibrated scan-simulation pipeline.
The scanned-split SER is close to the clean-split SER, which bounds
the residual gap on the augmentation distribution, but the
augmentation pipeline cannot reproduce every visual idiosyncrasy of
genuine Real Book paper: ink-pen artefacts, paper grain, show-through
from the back side of the page, and hand-written annotations are
absent from the synthetic distribution and are explicitly listed as
unimplemented in the project documentation.
The end-to-end measurement on real pages is therefore the most
important uncovered question.

\paragraph{Structural errors dominate the residual SER.}
About \SI{89}{\percent} of the residual edits are barline and tie
tokens (\Cref{sec:res-errors}).
These are largely musically irrelevant once a downstream renderer
re-bars the output, which is the reason for reporting a separate
melodic SER, but they remain the largest contributor to the headline
number and the most visible target for further improvement.
A grammar-aware decoder or a richer beat-counting validator could
reduce them further, at the cost of additional engineering.

\paragraph{The comparison to existing tools is qualitative.}
The system is positioned against prior work in \Cref{sec:res-soa}
through a published-numbers table (\Cref{tab:soa-comparison}) and a
head-to-head run against the open-source Audiveris engine on a genuine
Real Book scan (\Cref{fig:audiveris-vs-ours}), on which Audiveris
recovers a correct clef on only two of nine staves and no chord symbols,
while this system reconstructs the melody and the harmony.
That comparison is, however, qualitative and confined to a single page:
there is no shared ground-truth transcription, so no head-to-head error
rate is reported, and the commercial engines (SmartScore, PhotoScore)
were not run.
A quantitative, multi-page comparison against several engines would
further strengthen the case and remains future work.

\paragraph{Chord recognition sits outside the headline metric.}
The chord stream (\Cref{sec:impl-chord}) is trained and wired into the
pipeline, but its accuracy is reported on a separate track from the
melody model and is not folded into the SER figures of
\Cref{sec:conc-results}: the melody numbers are well characterised,
whereas the chord network is assessed with its own character-level
measure on a smaller set of strips. The headline results therefore
quantify the melodic half of the two-stream output far more thoroughly
than the harmonic half, and an end-to-end quality figure for the merged
JSON is not among the numbers reported here.

\paragraph{CTC-confidence reject gate is checkpoint-bound.}
The staff rejection gate of \Cref{sec:design-staff-reject} uses a
CTC mean log-probability threshold calibrated against the
distribution of the current checkpoint.
The threshold must be re-derived whenever the recogniser is
retrained, which is documented as a hard constraint in the
project's contributor guide.
The gate is robust within a checkpoint family but it is not
self-calibrating; a learned out-of-distribution detector would be a
cleaner long-term solution.

\paragraph{Single-page inference.}
The current pipeline processes only the first page of a multi-page
PDF (\Cref{sec:impl-inference}).
Batch multi-page handling is left to future work: it is a small
engineering task on top of the existing per-page entry point but
requires deciding how to expose multi-page output through the JSON
schema and the REST API.

\paragraph{Reading-order recovery within a system.}
Lead sheets occasionally use multi-staff systems with first- and
second-ending markings; the present pipeline detects staves
independently and does not recover their narrative reading order.
This is a layout-analysis problem that the morphological staff
detector is not designed to solve.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future-work}

Each extension below is scoped against the existing codebase and
infrastructure rather than as an open research direction.

\paragraph{Real-data fine-tuning and end-to-end measurement on Real
Book pages.}
The synthetic-to-real gap remains the most important open question.
A natural follow-up is to assemble a hand-annotated set of approximately \num{40}
staves drawn from real Real Book scans, run the fine-tuning protocol
specified in \Cref{sec:design-training} with the convolutional
backbone frozen and only the BiLSTM and CTC head updated, and
measure aggregate and melodic SER on the same pages before and after
fine-tuning.
The infrastructure (the music-strip labelling UI of
\Cref{sec:impl-api} and the \code{--finetune-data-dir} flag of the
training CLI) is already in place;
what is missing is the manual annotation effort, which is the binding
constraint.

\paragraph{Vocabulary extension to tuplets.}
Tuplets are absent from both the training distribution and the LMX
vocabulary, so the recogniser silently flattens triplet runs to plain
notes.
Adding tuplets would require introducing paired bracket tokens
(\code{tuplet:start:N:M}, \code{tuplet:stop}) into the grammar of
\Cref{sec:lmx-format}, regenerating the vocabulary file, retraining
the model from scratch, and extending the grammar fixer to track
in-flight tuplet groups.
The Real Book uses tuplets sparingly, so the expected gain is
moderate, but it is the single largest gap in the LMX vocabulary's
coverage of the corpus.

\paragraph{Polyphonic input via stem-direction voice separation.}
The current grammar and CTC loss assume a single voice per staff
(\Cref{sec:design-model}).
Extending the system to polyphonic input would require a richer LMX
vocabulary (chord-stack tokens, voice indicators) and either a
multi-target CTC formulation or a sequence-to-sequence
decoder~\cite{riosVilaSheetMusicTransformer2024}.
A pragmatic intermediate step is to detect stem direction at the
image level, split each staff into two monophonic substrips along the
voice boundary, and run the existing recogniser on each.
This trades model complexity for a more involved preprocessor and
keeps the trained checkpoint usable, at the cost of a known failure
mode on vertically stacked noteheads whose reading order is not
recoverable from the image alone.

\paragraph{Learned staff detection and full-page layout analysis.}
The morphological staff detector of \Cref{sec:design-staffdetect}
performs well on cleanly scanned Real Book pages but is fragile on
slanted staves and partial systems
\cite{simonettaOpticalMusicRecognition2024}.
Replacing it with a learned detector (YOLO-style box
regression~\cite{redmonYouOnlyLook2016} or a DETR-style
transformer detector~\cite{carionEndToEndObjectDetection2020}) trained on a few hundred
annotated pages would unlock multi-page batch inference and reduce the
failure rate on the hardest scans.
The current pipeline contract
(\Cref{tab:stage-contracts}) is exactly the interface the new detector
must implement, so the replacement is local: no downstream stage
needs to change.

\paragraph{Augmentation gap: paper grain and show-through.}
The augmentation pipeline does not model paper bleed-through or
realistic page-fibre texture (\Cref{sec:design-data}).
Adding these effects requires extending \code{augment\_scanned.py}
with a back-page blend (a low-opacity, horizontally-flipped second
image overlaid through a textured alpha mask) and a multi-octave
Perlin-noise paper-fibre layer applied after tone remapping.
This is a constrained engineering task within the existing
augmentation framework and would close the visible remainder of the
synthetic-to-real distribution shift on the noisier end of the
Real Book scan corpus.

\section{Technical Competences Achieved}
\label{sec:conc-competences}

This section maps the GEI/Computació technical competences declared at
TFG inscription onto the chapters and sections of this memoir where
evidence for each was developed.
Both declared competences are at the in-depth level.

\subsection{CCO1.1 — Algorithmic strategies and computational
performance}

\textbf{Statement.}
``To evaluate the computational complexity of a problem, know the
algorithmic strategies which can solve it and recommend, develop and
implement the solution which guarantees the best performance according
to the established requirements.'' (In depth.)

\textbf{Where evidence lives.}
The principal venue is the comparative analysis of architectural
alternatives in \Cref{ch:design}, where each major design decision is
adjudicated against rejected alternatives on complexity and
performance grounds. The pipeline decomposition weighs a modular
five-stage pipeline against monolithic and tree-decoder transformers
(\Cref{sec:design-pipeline,sec:design-pipeline-alts}); the
model-family decision weighs CRNN--CTC against autoregressive
transformers and pure-CNN classifiers on data efficiency, exposure
bias, and a fixed single-GPU compute budget
(\Cref{sec:design-model,sec:design-model-alts}); and the backbone
choice of ResNet18 is justified on parameter count, convergence rate,
and VRAM headroom.

The implementation chapter contributes further evidence. The
asymmetric-stride ResNet18 that preserves the CTC time axis at a
\(4\times\) compression ratio is described in \Cref{sec:impl-model}.
The beam-versus-greedy decoding trade-off, where width-5 beam search
saves 8 edits over \num{285958} tokens at $\approx 8.4\times$ the
decode cost, is measured in \Cref{ch:results} and restated in
\Cref{sec:conc-results}. The use of \code{multiprocessing} for the
CPU-bound data stages is set out in \Cref{sec:impl-data}, and the
choice of a deterministic stateful grammar walker over a constrained
decoder or finite-state transducer is argued in
\Cref{sec:design-grammar-alts}.

The acceptance criteria of \Cref{sec:design-domain} commit the
project to numeric targets ahead of measurement, and \Cref{ch:results}
adjudicates against them, closing the loop between the
algorithmic-strategy choices and the performance they deliver.

\subsection{CCO2.4 — Machine learning and extraction of information
from large data volumes}

\textbf{Statement.}
``To demonstrate knowledge and develop techniques about computational
learning; to design and implement applications and systems that use
them, including these ones dedicated to the automatic extraction of
information and knowledge from large data volumes.'' (In depth.)

\textbf{Where evidence lives.}
The machine-learning pipeline of this thesis is the principal
evidence. \Cref{ch:soa} surveys the OMR families and grounds the
architectural choice in published baselines; \Cref{sec:design-model}
commits to a CRNN--CTC stack supervised by the CTC
loss~\cite{gravesConnectionistTemporal2006} on LMX token sequences,
which learns the frame-to-token alignment without per-frame
supervision and underpins the melody-recognition stage.

The data dimension is large by Bachelor's-thesis standards. PrIMuS
contributes 87\,678 monophonic incipits, each re-rendered in LilyJAZZ
and paired with a synthetic scanned copy, yielding on the order of
\num{170000} training images after filtering
(\Cref{sec:design-data}). The design choices that exploit this volume
are developed elsewhere: synthetic supervision in place of manual
annotation, sample-level oversampling of rare tokens
(\Cref{sec:design-training}), virtual header injection for the
continuation-line case (\Cref{sec:impl-inference}), and a deterministic
split protocol that keeps validation and test disjoint
(\Cref{sec:impl-reproducibility}).

The structured-output side is satisfied by the choice of LMX as the
model's symbolic target, argued in
\Cref{sec:lmx-format,sec:design-lmx-alts}. The chord-OCR network
(\Cref{sec:impl-chord}) applies the same pattern to a different visual
language through synthetic pretraining followed by domain-targeted
fine-tuning on hand-labelled real strips. The full inference pipeline
(\Cref{sec:impl-inference}) closes the loop: it accepts an unseen
page, extracts the symbolic content as structured JSON, and serves the
result through a REST API (\Cref{sec:impl-api}).

\section{Methodological Reflection}
\label{sec:conc-reflection}

The work proceeded incrementally, one pipeline stage at a time, and
that order shaped the outcome. The synthetic-first decision was the
one that made a thesis-scale project workable: re-rendering PrIMuS in
the target engraving style cost a one-off LilyPond setup and a few
thousand CPU-hours, but it produced a labelled corpus far larger than
any manual annotation effort could have. Pairing that synthetic
supervision with a calibrated augmentation pipeline brought the
scanned-split SER within \num{0.1} percentage points of the
clean-split SER, the most uncertain outcome at the outset.

Building the system as discrete stages with a clean contract between
them (\Cref{tab:stage-contracts}) made each stage debuggable in
isolation and kept iteration cheap; the deterministic grammar fixer,
a few hundred lines of stateful code, removed a class of structural
errors that would otherwise have cost model capacity. With hindsight
the chord-OCR stream was scoped too late, after the melody network had
converged, and developing the two streams in parallel would have given
the chord fine-tuning loop more iteration time.
