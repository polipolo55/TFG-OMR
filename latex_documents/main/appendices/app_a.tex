\section{Earned-Value Budget Control}
\label{app:evm}

This section gives the earned-value-management (EVM) definitions
summarised in \Cref{sec:mgmt-control}.
Each sprint is characterised by three quantities: the
\textit{budgeted cost of work scheduled} (BCWS), the planned cost of
the tasks the sprint should have completed; the \textit{budgeted cost
of work performed} (BCWP), the planned cost of the tasks actually
completed; and the \textit{actual cost of work performed} (ACWP), the
real cost incurred to complete those tasks.
The three control indicators are derived from these primitives.

\textit{Cost variance.}
$\mathrm{CV} = \mathrm{BCWP} - \mathrm{ACWP}$ is the difference
between the budgeted cost of completed work and what it actually
cost.
A positive CV means the sprint came in under budget and a negative
CV means it overran; this is the headline cost-health indicator for
a single sprint.

\textit{Schedule variance.}
$\mathrm{SV} = \mathrm{BCWP} - \mathrm{BCWS}$ is the difference
between the work completed and the work planned, both measured in
the common currency of budgeted cost.
A positive SV means the sprint completed more than was planned and a
negative SV means it fell behind.
SV is reported alongside CV because cost and schedule can move in
opposite directions: a sprint can come in under cost simply by
not completing all its work, which CV alone would not catch.

\textit{Cost performance index.}
$\mathrm{CPI} = \mathrm{BCWP} / \mathrm{ACWP}$ is the
dimensionless ratio of value earned to cost incurred.
A CPI of 1.0 indicates exact agreement with the plan, below 1.0
indicates an overrun, and above 1.0 indicates an underrun.
The advantage of the index form over the raw variance is that it
is directly comparable across sprints of different sizes.

\subsection*{Human-resources rate derivation}

Each task is costed at the loaded rate of the role that
predominantly performs it: the GEP tasks (T0.1--T0.4), the supervisor
meetings (T0.5), and the end-of-sprint reviews (T1.5, T2.5, T3.5,
T4.5) at the Project Manager rate; the literature survey, dataset
exploration, and error-analysis tasks at the Research Analyst rate;
the dataset-rendering, model, and pipeline tasks at the ML Engineer
rate; and the thesis-writing tasks (T0.6, T5.4) together with defence
preparation (T5.5) at the Technical Writer rate.
Aggregating the task hours by role gives \num{92}\,h Project Manager,
\num{72}\,h Research Analyst, \num{255}\,h ML Engineer, and
\num{90}\,h Technical Writer (\num{509}\,h in total), the allocation
shown in \Cref{tab:roles-cost}.
Summing the corresponding costs yields the \EUR{}13\,615.57
human-resources total, which divided by the 509 hours gives a
project-weighted blended rate of \EUR{}26.75/h; this differs slightly
from the unweighted mean of the four rates (\EUR{}26.27/h) because the
ML~Engineer role, the most expensive after the Project Manager,
carries half the project's hours.

\subsection*{Hardware amortisation}

The general-costs hardware line is a four-year linear amortisation
prorated for the project duration.
The workstation---a Ryzen~7 7800X3D with 32\,GB of DDR5 memory and an
NVIDIA RTX~3060 12\,GB---is valued at \EUR{}1\,000, the monitor at
\EUR{}200, and the peripherals at \EUR{}45, for a \EUR{}1\,245 base.
Charged linearly over a four-year, \num{208}-week useful life for the
21 project weeks, this gives
$\EUR{}1\,245\times 21/208 \approx \EUR{}125.69$.
