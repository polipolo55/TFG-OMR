1     Introduction and Context
1.1   Academic/Professional Framework
This will be an academic first project in the context of the TFG at FIB-UPC meaning its part
of modality A, which is a requirement for the completion of the degree in Computer Science
at the Universitat Politècnica de Catalunya (UPC). The project is supervised by Manel Frigola
Bourlon, a professor at the Facultat d’Informàtica de Barcelona (FIB), and guided by Xavier
Morales Sorolla, who serves as the GEP tutor. The TFG is part of the curriculum for the
Computació specialty and was chosen by me personally due to my interest in the intersection of
music and technology, as well as the potential applications of Optical Music Recognition (OMR)
in various fields such as music education, digital archiving, and music information retrieval.

1.2   Domain Concepts and Terminology
Optical Music Recognition (OMR): The process of automatically converting scanned or
photographed music sheets into machine-readable symbolic formats (e.g., MusicXML, MIDI).
    Music Sheet/Score: A printed or digital document containing musical notation, including
notes, staves, time signatures, and other musical symbols.
    End-to-End System: A unified approach that processes input directly to output without
intermediate manual steps, typically using a single neural network or pipeline.
    Music Information Retrieval (MIR): The interdisciplinary field concerned with extract-
ing meaningful information from music data.
    Symbolic Notation: Machine-readable representation of music (e.g., MusicXML, MIDI) as
opposed to audio waveforms.
    Digital Archiving: The process of converting and preserving documents in digital form for
long-term accessibility.
    Apart from the clear concepts this project will heavily rely on the knowleadge of general
music theory, such as the understanding of musical notation, rhythm, and structure, as well
as the technical concepts related to deep learning, computer vision, and data processin. When
necessary these concepts will be defined and explained in the context of the project to ensure
clarity and accessibility for readers who may not be familiar with all the technical details.

1.3   Problem Statement
As a musician myself, I have often encountered the challenge of digitizing music sheets for
various purposes, such as sharing with others, creating digital archives, or using music software
for composition and analysis but specially as a practise tool, being handed a physical music
sheet that must be played with a band live without rehersal (really common in some spheres).
In such dynamic environments, physical paper restricts flexibility. Musicians frequently need to
instantly transpose the chart for a different instrument, generate an immediate audio playback
to hear the groove before playing, or integrate the sheet into a digital library on a tablet. The
manual process of transcribing music sheets into digital formats is time-consuming and prone to
errors. Therefore, there is a need for an efficient and accurate method to convert physical music
sheets into machine-readable formats.

1.4   Stakeholders
Musicians: Individuals who read and perform music sheets, benefiting from digital formats
for practice, performance, and sharing. Music Educators: Teachers who can use digital music
sheets for instructional purposes and to provide students with interactive learning materials. Mu-
sic Archivists: Professionals responsible for preserving and organizing music collections, who
