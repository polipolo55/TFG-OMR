\chapter{Results and Evaluation}
\label{ch:results}

This chapter reports the quantitative and qualitative results obtained
with the system described in \Cref{ch:design,ch:impl}.
\Cref{sec:res-protocol} fixes the experimental protocol (datasets,
splits, hardware, metrics).
\Cref{sec:res-training} presents the training trajectory of the
synthetic-data baseline.
\Cref{sec:res-ser} reports aggregate Symbol Error Rate (SER) on the
held-out synthetic test split and analyses the per-sample error
distribution.
\Cref{sec:res-errors} decomposes the errors by edit operation and by
token category, showing that nearly all of the residual error budget
falls on \code{measure} and tie tokens rather than on pitch or duration.
\Cref{sec:res-ablations} isolates two of the main design decisions ---
the backbone family and the scan-augmentation pipeline --- by
re-training with each choice reverted in turn.
\Cref{sec:res-qualitative} shows qualitative examples drawn from the
perfect and near-median tails of the SER distribution; further
worst-case predictions are collected in \Cref{app:qual}.
\Cref{sec:res-soa} positions these numbers against the published
state of the art.
\Cref{sec:res-domaingap} addresses the synthetic-to-real domain gap
and identifies the main open experimental question: measuring real-domain
melody SER once enough ground-truth pages are available.

\section{Experimental Protocol}
\label{sec:res-protocol}

All quantitative SER numbers in this chapter are produced from the
synthetic-data baseline at its best epoch (epoch 83 of the 90-epoch run;
\Cref{sec:res-training}), with the hyperparameters listed in
\Cref{tab:hyperparams-omr} (\Cref{app:hyperparams}). This distinction is
deliberate: the baseline is the model with a clean held-out synthetic
test protocol, whereas the real-data fine-tuned model deployed as
\code{models/latest/best\_model.pt} (\Cref{sec:res-finetune}) has no
held-out real melody set large enough for a stable SER. On the synthetic
validation split, the fine-tuned checkpoint remains within run-to-run
noise of the baseline. The procedure for reproducing the baseline
numbers is shown at the end of this section.

\paragraph{Datasets and splits.}
The corpora, the three domain filters, and the deterministic
\code{make\_splits} partition are specified in
\Cref{sec:design-data}. The three filters are applied before the
split. Clean and scanned variants of the same source share a split
assignment, so the resulting 4\,608-sample test split reports the same
musical content under two visual regimes.

\paragraph{Hardware and software.}
The hardware and software stack is described in \Cref{ch:impl}.
On that single-GPU configuration, the full 90-epoch run takes
approximately 13\,h of wall time ($\approx 8.6$\,min/epoch),
measured from the training log of the selected checkpoint.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
The headline metric is the token-level \term{Symbol Error Rate} (SER)
defined in \Cref{sec:soa-metrics} --- total edit distance over the test
set divided by total ground-truth
length~\cite{levenshteinBinaryCodes1966,hajicOMRMetricsEvaluation2019} --- reported
on both the clean and scanned splits. The \term{melodic SER} (the same
measure after stripping the \code{measure} and tie tokens) and the
\term{perfect-transcription rate} (fraction of samples decoded with
zero edits) follow the definitions in \Cref{sec:design-eval}.

\paragraph{Repetitions.}
Numbers reported here come from a single training run, since each run
costs $\approx 13$\,h on the available hardware.
The training curve in \Cref{fig:training-curves} is smooth and the
validation SER plateau is tight (within roughly $\pm 0.02$ percentage
points over the last ten epochs), which suggests the point estimates
below are dominated by the data distribution rather than by
optimisation noise or the choice of a single seed.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
The entire test-set evaluation is reproduced by
%
\begin{quote}
\code{poetry run python scripts/evaluate\_full.py}\\
\code{\quad --checkpoint models/run\_20260612\_101637/best\_model.pt}\\
\code{\quad --split test --both-splits}
\end{quote}
%
which runs greedy decoding on the clean and scanned variants of the
held-out split and prints both the aggregate and the melodic SER.

\section{Training Trajectory}
\label{sec:res-training}

\Cref{fig:training-curves} shows the trajectory of the CTC loss on
the train and validation splits and the corresponding validation SER
over the 90 epochs of the training run; the best validation SER was
reached at epoch 83 and the run continued to the 90-epoch cap
without the patience-12 early stopping ever triggering.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_training_curves}
    \caption{Training trajectory of the synthetic-data baseline.
    \textbf{Left:} CTC loss on the train (blue) and validation
    (orange) splits.
    The train loss continues to decrease while the validation loss
    plateaus around \num{0.05}--\num{0.06}, indicating a small but
    bounded overfit margin.
    \textbf{Right:} in-loop validation SER over the same epochs,
    computed once per epoch boundary by the training harness.
    The curve drops sharply from $\approx \SI{12}{\percent}$ to
    $\approx \SI{2}{\percent}$ within seven epochs and converges to
    $\approx \SI{1.2}{\percent}$ by the end of training.
    The best checkpoint is marked at epoch 83.
    This in-loop estimate is consistent with the standalone evaluator,
    which yields \SI{1.23}{\percent} on the scanned test split
    (see \Cref{sec:res-ser}).}
    \label{fig:training-curves}
\end{figure}

Validation SER converges much faster than the loss curve suggests: by
epoch 7 it is already at $\approx \SI{2}{\percent}$, and the remaining
epochs of training produce a further reduction of only
$\approx 0.9$ percentage points.
In these runs the model appears to learn most of the CTC alignment
early, with late training refining a small fraction of difficult
samples.
The train/val loss gap remains small throughout, consistent with the
regularisation choices justified in \Cref{sec:design-model}; the
trained weight distributions, reported as a sanity check in
\Cref{app:weights}, show no sign of degenerate or dead components.

The in-loop validation SER ($\approx\SI{1.2}{\percent}$ at convergence)
is an epoch-boundary estimate computed by the training harness on the
validation split.
Standalone re-evaluation of the same checkpoint with the single-process
evaluator gives \SI{1.23}{\percent} on the scanned split, in close
agreement with the in-loop figure and with the test-set numbers in
\Cref{sec:res-ser}.
The training curve is therefore read as a \emph{convergence trace};
\Cref{tab:results-headline} has the authoritative numbers.

\section{Aggregate Symbol Error Rate}
\label{sec:res-ser}

Evaluation is performed on the 4\,608-sample held-out test split with
greedy CTC decoding.
Aggregate SER is \SI{1.23}{\percent} on the scanned variant and
\SI{1.17}{\percent} on the clean variant; melodic SER is
\SI{0.14}{\percent} and \SI{0.10}{\percent} respectively.
The perfect-transcription rate is \SI{72.7}{\percent} on the scanned
variant and \SI{73.7}{\percent} on the clean variant.
The two variants are very close: the clean one is marginally ahead on
aggregate SER (by \num{0.06} percentage points) and on
perfect-transcription rate, as expected, since the scan-simulated
inputs add visual distortion the clean renders do not carry.  The gap
is small because the model is trained throughout on scan-augmented
inputs (\Cref{sec:impl-data}), so the two test distributions sit close
together.  The augmentation pipeline meets its acceptance criterion of
keeping the clean-to-scanned gap below one percentage point
(\Cref{sec:design-domain}).
\Cref{tab:results-headline} consolidates these headline numbers, which
the rest of the chapter decomposes, and \Cref{fig:ser-distribution}
shows the corresponding per-sample SER distribution on the scanned
variant.

The modal outcome is a perfect transcription
($\approx \SI{72.7}{\percent}$ of samples have SER \num{0}), and the
empirical CDF exceeds \num{0.90} by SER \num{0.05}.
The distribution is bimodal, a tall spike at zero plus a longer right
tail: in these experiments the model tends either to read a sample
correctly or to fail on a specific localised pattern.
The samples in the right tail are analysed in
\Cref{sec:res-qualitative,app:qual}.

A complementary melodic SER is computed on the same edit distance
after stripping the \code{measure} and the two tie tokens
(see \Cref{tab:results-headline}).
The checkpoint sits roughly an order of magnitude lower on this
metric than on the aggregate SER for both visual variants
(\SI{0.14}{\percent} scanned, \SI{0.10}{\percent} clean), so the bulk
of the residual error is structural rather than melodic.
The error decomposition in \Cref{sec:res-errors} explains why.

\paragraph{Beam search.}
A beam-search ablation with widths $\{1, 5, 10\}$ is reproducible with
\code{poetry run python src/cli.py evaluate-ab --beams 1,5,10}.
In earlier testing, beam search reduced the edit count by well under
\num{0.01} percentage points of SER at roughly $8\times$ the
wall-clock decode time, with width 10 offering no improvement over
width 5 --- consistent with the near-deterministic posteriors of a
monophonic CTC model.
Greedy decoding is therefore the default used for every number in
this chapter, and beam search is retained as an opt-in flag at
inference time, consistent with the model documentation in
\Cref{sec:design-model}.

\paragraph{Inference latency.}
End-to-end per-page latency was measured on the target RTX~3060 over a
representative synthetic page ($2550\times3300$\,px, eight staves) with
greedy decoding.
The full \code{run\_pipeline} call completes in a median of
\SI{114}{\milli\second} (mean \SI{114}{\milli\second}~$\pm$~\SI{1}{\milli\second}
over ten repetitions).
\Cref{tab:latency} breaks this down by stage: staff detection and
preprocessing dominate the budget, the two CRNN forward passes are
comparatively cheap, and the deterministic grammar fixer is
negligible.
The per-stage rows are a single representative run and sum to about
\SI{81}{\milli\second}; the rest of the end-to-end median is fixed
\code{run\_pipeline} overhead, chiefly PNG-encoding the annotated page
returned in the response.
A standalone, unbatched per-strip measurement on a single wide staff
($\approx\SI{1500}{px}$) gives a median CRNN decode time of
\SI{5.2}{\milli\second} (greedy, 20 repetitions); the lower per-staff
cost implied by \Cref{tab:latency} reflects batching the page's staves
into one forward pass.

\section{Error Decomposition}
\label{sec:res-errors}

\subsection{Edit-operation breakdown}

The aggregate edit count over the test split decomposes into the three
Levenshtein operations: \term{deletions}, \term{insertions} and
\term{substitutions}.
\Cref{fig:edit-dist} shows the distribution on the scanned variant.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.92\textwidth]{figures/fig_error_pie}
    \caption{Distribution of the \num{3506} edit operations over the
    scanned test split, as a share of the total.
    Deletions and insertions together account for $\approx\SI{97}{\percent}$
    of the edit budget; substitutions are a small minority.
    This imbalance is characteristic of structural-token errors
    (missed or spurious barlines), not of incorrect symbol
    recognition.}
    \label{fig:edit-dist}
\end{figure}

Substitutions are the rarest error type at \SI{3.4}{\percent} of the
\num{3506} total edits, which matters because substitutions are the
errors that affect musical \textit{content}.
Insertions (\SI{38.8}{\percent}) and deletions (\SI{57.8}{\percent})
dominate; as the next subsection shows, the majority of those concern
the \code{measure} token.

\subsection{Per-token breakdown}

The breakdown is unambiguous: \code{measure} dominates both deletions
(\num{1562} occurrences, an order of magnitude above the
next-most-deleted token) and insertions (\num{1128}).
Tie tokens (\code{tied:start} and \code{tied:stop}) follow in second
place for both categories, contributing \num{291} deletions and \num{148}
insertions in total.
Together, the \code{measure} and tie tokens account for approximately
\SI{90}{\percent} of the total edit budget.
Pitch and octave tokens are essentially absent from the deletion and
insertion lists; they surface only among the substitutions
(\Cref{tab:top-subs}), at low frequencies per token
over thousands of test samples.

These numbers are consistent with the design rationale in
\Cref{sec:design-grammar}.
The substitutions that do occur (\num{119} in total) are spread across
duration confusions between visually similar values
($\rm{eighth} \leftrightarrow 16^{\rm th}$,
$\rm{eighth} \leftrightarrow \rm{quarter}$), pitch confusions between
neighbouring letters (e.g.\ $\rm{pitch:B} \to \rm{pitch:G}$,
$\rm{pitch:A} \to \rm{pitch:B}$), and occasional octave
(e.g.\ $\rm{octave:6} \to \rm{octave:5}$) or accidental slips.
A handful of substitutions are themselves structural --- an accidental
or barline read as the other (e.g.\ $\rm{flat} \to \code{measure}$) ---
rather than melodic.
The residual error budget is therefore structural and rhythmic, not
melodic.

\Cref{tab:top-subs} lists the most frequent substitution pairs on the
scanned test split.  The leading pair (a pitch confusion) occurs eight
times and the rest tail off quickly, confirming that no single
content-level confusion dominates the budget.

\subsection{SER versus sequence length}

A natural question is whether long sequences accumulate more errors
than short ones, since the CTC time-step budget is proportional to
image width and there is no \textit{a priori} guarantee that long
samples remain in alignment.
\Cref{fig:ser-vs-length} plots this relationship directly.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_ser_vs_length}
    \caption{Per-sample SER as a function of ground-truth sequence
    length on the scanned test split.
    Orange diamonds mark per-length median values, which sit
    essentially on zero across the entire length range.
    The blue cloud above the baseline shows the long-tail samples
    whose SER lies above the per-length median; there is a mild
    increase in tail thickness around \num{120}-token sequences but
    no systematic divergence at long lengths.
    The CTC alignment holds over the full range of
    sequence lengths in the corpus.}
    \label{fig:ser-vs-length}
\end{figure}

The median per-sample SER is essentially zero across the full range of
sequence lengths in the corpus (\num{20} to \num{140} tokens).
The tail of the distribution broadens slightly at the longest
lengths, but the model does not exhibit the alignment
breakdown that CTC can occasionally produce when the input is too
short for the target.
This confirms that the $W/4$ width-compression factor of the CNN
backbone (\Cref{sec:design-model}) is appropriate for the sequence
lengths the system encounters.

\section{Ablations}
\label{sec:res-ablations}

Several of the design decisions in \Cref{ch:design} can be turned into
direct experiments by re-training with one choice reverted and
everything else fixed.
Three such ablations were scoped: backbone family
(\Cref{sec:design-model}), scan-augmentation pipeline
(\Cref{sec:design-data}), and virtual header injection for
continuation staves (\Cref{sec:impl-inference}).
The first two are reported below with results.
The last requires a full re-training run
($\approx 13$\,h on the RTX~3060) that was not completed within the
project timeline; it is left as a planned experiment whose expected
outcome follows from the design arguments in \Cref{ch:design}.

A VGG backbone was evaluated in an earlier configuration of the
pipeline (smaller corpus, an earlier iteration of the scan-augmentation
pipeline) and reached an aggregate SER comparable to the ResNet18
baseline at the same epoch budget, but required approximately twice
the wall-clock training time on the same GPU.
The comparison is not perfectly controlled against the headline run
--- the dataset and augmentation differed --- but the efficiency gap
is large enough that a matched re-run is unlikely to reverse it:
VGG's additional convolutional width does not reduce the residual
error at this scale, while its higher parameter count meaningfully
increases both training time and GPU memory pressure.
The ResNet18 default is therefore retained on efficiency grounds,
consistent with the argument in \Cref{sec:design-model-alts}.

The scan-augmentation ablation re-trains the same architecture with
\code{use\_scanned=False} (clean LilyJAZZ renders only, no scan
simulation) and evaluates on both held-out test variants.
In an earlier configuration this ablation showed the expected pattern:
a no-augmentation checkpoint stayed competitive on the clean split but
its scanned-split SER rose by roughly an order of magnitude, the
clean-to-scanned gap widening from a fraction of a percentage point to
several points.
The scan-simulation pipeline is therefore not a regulariser on clean
data but a prerequisite for robustness to the visual distribution
shift the system faces at real-scan inference, directly supporting the
design choice justified in \Cref{sec:design-data}.

\section{Comparison to the State of the Art}
\label{sec:res-soa}

A direct numerical comparison to prior work is not entirely fair,
because each published OMR system targets a different corpus, label
schema and evaluation protocol.
What is reported below is therefore a positioning rather than a
benchmark.

The closest direct point of comparison is Calvo-Zaragoza et\,al.'s
CRNN--CTC baseline on PrIMuS, which established the CTC-on-staff
formulation as a credible approach to end-to-end staff-line
recognition~\cite{calvoZaragozaEndToEndNeural2018,calvoZaragozaCameraPrimus2018}.
The model here uses the same family of architectures and a derived
PrIMuS corpus, and achieves an aggregate SER of $\approx\SI{1.2}{\percent}$
on a comparable synthetic split, of the same order as that baseline;
the contribution is not a better number on PrIMuS but
the addition of the LilyJAZZ render, the scan-simulation
augmentation pipeline (\Cref{sec:design-data}) and the deterministic
grammar fixer (\Cref{sec:design-grammar}), all aimed at the Real Book
domain specifically.

Transformer-based OMR systems
\cite{riosVilaSheetMusicTransformer2024,riosVilaSMTPlusPlus2024,mayerPracticalEndToEnd2024,yangLEGATO2025}
have since pushed published SER lower on broader corpora, but at the
cost of training-time and inference-time budgets that are out of
reach for the hardware envelope of this project (RTX 3060, 12\,GB).
The CRNN--CTC choice was justified in \Cref{sec:design-model-alts} on
the basis of this budget; the numbers above show the resulting system
is competitive on the narrow lead-sheet domain it was scoped to.

The Audiveris~\cite{audiverisAudiveris} symbolic OMR engine is the
canonical free-software baseline.  A side-by-side qualitative
comparison on a genuine Real Book page is the natural way to position
this system against an established off-the-shelf tool.

\Cref{tab:soa-comparison} consolidates this positioning across the
three reference families and the present system.
The table is read down the rows, not across them: each system targets a
different corpus and, in several cases, reports a different primary
metric, so the error column is not a like-for-like ranking.
The one column that does compare directly is voicing and compute
envelope, where the contrast the project was scoped around is clear ---
the present system reaches $\approx\SI{1.2}{\percent}$ SER on its
monophonic target within a single-GPU, roughly half-day training
budget, whereas the transformer systems that report lower error on
broader corpora do so at a training and inference cost an order of
magnitude larger.

signature on any system and extracts no chord symbols at all.
The exported MusicXML, re-rendered to notation in

the harmony is absent.
These failures are specific to the hand-lettered engraving rather than
to the tool's configuration: Audiveris is built for, and performs
adequately on, cleanly typeset scores (\Cref{sec:soa-classical}), but
the rounded hand-drawn noteheads, free-hand clefs, and dense chord text
of a Real Book page fall outside the operating envelope its
template-matching front-end assumes.

every recognised staff is rendered on the correct treble clef, and the
chord stream recovers \num{36} symbols across the page, the

\code{G-7 C7 Fmaj7}, \dots) reading correctly.

transcription --- but the output is far more usable, a difference that
comes from the two design choices Audiveris does not
make: virtual header injection for clef-less staves
(\Cref{sec:impl-inference}) and a dedicated chord-recognition stream
(\Cref{sec:design-twostream}).

\section{The Synthetic-to-Real Domain Gap}
\label{sec:res-domaingap}

The numbers reported above are all measured on the held-out
\textit{synthetic} test split.
The question of practical importance is whether the same model
generalises to scans of genuine Real Book pages, the input distribution
the system will face once deployed.
The fine-tuning experiment of \Cref{sec:res-finetune} answers this
qualitatively and supplies the deployed model; a \textit{quantitative}
real-domain SER, both zero-shot and after fine-tuning, is the part that
remains open, gated on a larger hand-labelled set.

\subsection{Fine-tuning on real pages}
\label{sec:res-finetune}

The two-phase plan of \Cref{sec:design-training} also calls for a
real-data phase on the melody stream.  That phase was carried out, and
the resulting checkpoint is the model now deployed by the pipeline.  The
evidence it produces is weaker than for the chord branch, for a reason
that follows from the leakage-safe split design rather than from the
method itself.

\paragraph{Annotation set.}
\num{36} real staves were hand-labelled with the model-assisted
music-strip UI of \Cref{sec:impl-api}, correcting the synthetic model's
first-pass prediction rather than transcribing from scratch.  The strips
were harvested from \num{13} scanned Real Book pages; a further
\num{212} candidate strips were skipped as illegible or non-lead-sheet
(title bands, attribution lines, multi-staff systems), an
$\approx\SI{85}{\percent}$ discard rate that doubles as a measure of the
staff detector's precision on raw pages.  Of the \num{36} kept staves,
\num{7} carry a printed header and \num{29} are continuation staves
labelled header-less, matching the inference contract of
\Cref{sec:design-pipeline}.

\paragraph{Protocol.}
Fine-tuning is a warm start rather than a fresh run: the converged
synthetic checkpoint (epoch \num{83}) is loaded with the
\code{--init-weights} flag described in \Cref{sec:impl-finetune}, which
restores the weights only and then begins a fresh OneCycle schedule at a
reduced peak learning rate of $1{\times}10^{-4}$ for \num{12} epochs.
The \num{36} real staves are appended to the \emph{training} split alone
through \code{--finetune-data-dir}; validation and test stay drawn from
the synthetic corpus, so the held-out synthetic numbers remain directly
comparable to the baseline.  This is the melody analogue of the chord
fine-tuning of \Cref{sec:res-chord}, with one structural difference noted
below.

\paragraph{Result.}
On the synthetic validation split the fine-tuned checkpoint is
indistinguishable from the baseline: validation SER moves from
\SI{1.18}{\percent} to \SI{1.19}{\percent}, inside run-to-run noise.  The
\num{36} real staves are roughly \SI{0.1}{\percent} of the
\num{36873}-sample training pool, far too few to shift a synthetic
metric, and the experiment does not expect them to: what it confirms is
that fine-tuning causes no measurable regression on the synthetic
distribution, that is, no catastrophic forgetting.  On genuine scans the
effect is visible.  Run on three genuine Real Book sheets that were not part
of the annotation set, the fine-tuned checkpoint produced cleaner
transcriptions than the synthetic baseline on all three, with fewer
spurious duration and accidental tokens and better-aligned barlines.

\paragraph{Why no held-out real SER.}
The chord branch reserved \num{29} of its real strips for a quantitative
held-out test (\Cref{tab:chord-eval}); the melody fine-tune set is
instead appended in full to the training split.  This is a deliberate
consequence of the leakage-safe split of \Cref{sec:design-training}:
real staves are kept out of the synthetic validation and test pools so
that no real sample can leak into evaluation, but with only \num{36}
staves there are too few to also carve out a real held-out split that
would give a stable SER.  The melody real-domain result is therefore
qualitative at this scale, and the measurable
$\geq\SI{30}{\percent}$-reduction target of Objective~7 awaits the larger
annotated set discussed in \Cref{sec:future-work}.

\subsection{Qualitative inference on a complete Real Book page}
\label{sec:res-realpage}

Although quantitative real-domain melody SER still awaits a larger
annotation set, the full pipeline can be inspected qualitatively on a
genuine scanned Real Book page, the input distribution the system is
ultimately meant for.
\Cref{fig:realpage-annotated} shows the end-to-end run on the
\textit{Satin Doll} lead sheet.
Staff detection finds nine candidate systems; the two-tier reject gate
(\Cref{sec:design-staff-reject}) discards the title band (its
staff-like rules are too irregularly spaced) and the bottom attribution
line (rejected on OCR text density), and the remaining seven music
staves are recognised, each annotated with its detected chord symbols
(\num{36} across the page).
\Cref{fig:realpage-staff} pairs one recognised staff with the
LilyPond re-render of its predicted LMX.

This is qualitative evidence only --- the page carries no ground-truth
transcription, so no SER is reported, and the quantitative real-domain
measurement remains the missing experiment.
What it does show is that the melody recogniser and the chord stream
both produce clef-correct, harmonically sensible output on a real
hand-lettered scan, not just on the synthetic split, and that the
reject gate behaves as designed on genuine page furniture.
The recovered chords are the correct changes of the tune, and the
rendered melody follows the contour of the input staff.
The two rejected regions are both non-music: the title band at the top
and the attribution line at the bottom, both correctly filtered by the
gate (\Cref{sec:design-staff-reject}).

\subsection{Chord Recognition}
\label{sec:res-chord}

The chord stream is evaluated on its own track, because its training
data, vocabulary, and metric all differ from the music CRNN
(\Cref{sec:impl-chord}).
The reported metric is the \term{character error rate} (CER), the
Levenshtein edit distance over the predicted character string
normalised by the reference length, together with the
\term{exact-match} rate (fraction of strips decoded with no character
error).  The \num{29}-class chord vocabulary is character-level, so CER
here plays the role that SER plays for the melody network.
\Cref{tab:chord-eval} consolidates the three evaluation conditions; the
numbers are reproduced by \code{scripts/evaluate\_chord.py}.

The synthetic pre-trained model is effectively perfect on clean synthetic
strips (\SI{0.0}{\percent} CER, every one of the \num{2000} validation
strips decoded exactly), but it collapses on real strips when applied
zero-shot: \SI{74.0}{\percent} CER over the \num{291} hand-labelled
strips, with not a single strip decoded correctly.  The dominant
zero-shot error is spurious insertion (the model hallucinates extra
characters on the unfamiliar hand-lettered glyphs and page clutter
rather than substituting one character for another), which is the chord
analogue of the synthetic-to-real gap framed for the melody stream
above, but far more severe: the chord glyphs of a genuine Real Book page
diverge from the LilyJAZZ renders more sharply than staff notation
does, and the chord strip additionally carries binder-hole shadows,
staff-line bleed, and slash-repeat marks (\Cref{sec:impl-chord}).

Fine-tuning on the small real set closes most of that gap.  On the
\num{29} held-out real strips the same architecture, after fine-tuning
on the other \num{262} real strips, drops from \SI{69.9}{\percent} to
\SI{5.9}{\percent} CER and reaches a \SI{58.6}{\percent} exact-match
rate (17 of 29 strips decoded with zero character errors).  This is the
direct chord-stream evidence for the synthetic pre-training then
real fine-tuning plan of \Cref{sec:design-training}: for the chord
branch, unlike the melody branch, the real fine-tuning phase is not an
optional refinement but a prerequisite for usable accuracy.

The residual errors of the deployed (fine-tuned) model are
unsystematic and concentrated in the stylised quality glyphs: the
spelled-out diminished suffix is occasionally dropped
(\code{Abdim7}\,$\to$\,\code{Ab7}), the altered-dominant extension digit
is confused (\code{7\#9}\,$\to$\,\code{7\#1}), the augmented sign is read
as a sharp (\code{F+7}\,$\to$\,\code{F\#7}), and a handful of root
letters are mis-read.  These match the stylised-glyph failure modes
anticipated when the two-stream design rejected general-purpose OCR
(\Cref{sec:design-twostream-alts}).

\paragraph{Caveat.}
The held-out real chord set is only \num{29} strips, so the
fine-tuned percentages above carry wide confidence intervals and are
reported with their raw counts.  Enlarging the hand-labelled real set
is the same annotation-bound constraint that gates the melody
domain-gap experiment at the head of this section.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:res-summary}

The synthetic-data baseline trained from scratch reaches the headline
figures of \Cref{tab:results-headline}: aggregate SER of
$\approx\SI{1.2}{\percent}$ on both the clean and the scan-augmented test
splits, melodic SER an order of magnitude lower, and a
perfect-transcription rate of $\approx\SI{73}{\percent}$.
The residual error budget is concentrated in the \code{measure} and
tie tokens, which together account for $\approx \SI{90}{\percent}$ of
all edits, while pitch and octave errors are negligible.
Combined with the deterministic grammar fixer that reconstructs
barline boundaries from the time signature, these results place the
synthetic baseline within the operating range required for downstream
musical applications.

The chord-recognition stream, evaluated separately in \Cref{sec:res-chord},
confirms the same pre-training then fine-tuning pattern at the character
level: a zero-shot CER of \SI{69.9}{\percent} on the held-out real split drops
to \SI{5.9}{\percent} after fine-tuning on the other \num{262} hand-labelled
strips, with a \SI{58.6}{\percent} exact-match rate.

Two experimental fronts remain open.
First, the synthetic-to-real domain gap for the music stream
(\Cref{sec:res-domaingap}): the melody fine-tuning phase has been run
and deployed and improves transcription on genuine scans qualitatively
(\Cref{sec:res-finetune}), but a quantitative held-out real SER still
awaits a larger annotated set, which is what keeps the verdict on
Objective~7 at \enquote{partially met} in \Cref{ch:conclusions}.
Second, one of the three ablations --- virtual header injection
(\Cref{sec:res-ablations}) --- requires an additional training run that
was not completed within the project timeline; the backbone and
scan-augmentation ablations reported above already cover the two choices
most central to the design.
