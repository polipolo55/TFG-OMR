\chapter{Sustainability and Social Commitment}
\label{ch:sustain}

This chapter records the sustainability dimension of the project,
following the matrix and reflection framework set out in the
sustainability guide of the FIB project-management course (GEP,
\textit{Gesti\'o de Projectes})~\cite{gepSustainabilityReport2018}, in
accordance with the requirement of Article~12 of the TFG normativa
(Acord Consell de Govern UPC 07/2023).
It opens with the self-assessment that started the project, fills the
three-dimensional sustainability matrix (economic, environmental,
and social) for both the design-and-development stage and the
anticipated useful life, records the ethical and open-science
position adopted, and revisits the assessment once the work is
complete.
The discussion is anchored to four UN Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) that the project plausibly
touches: SDG~4 (Quality Education), SDG~9 (Industry, Innovation and
Infrastructure), SDG~10 (Reduced Inequalities), and SDG~12
(Responsible Consumption and Production).
The relevant goal is named within each dimension below.
The chapter also consolidates the applicable-regulations,
intellectual-property, and personal-data analysis for the whole
thesis (\Cref{sec:sust-commitment}), which is referenced
forward from \Cref{ch:mgmt} but discussed only here.

\subsection{Economic dimension}
\label{sec:sust-economic}

\textbf{PPP (design and development).}
The total project cost (\EUR{}16\,097.16) is constructed in detail
in \Cref{sec:mgmt-budget}; what matters for the sustainability
reading is the structure of that budget rather than the headline
number.
The expenditure is dominated by specialised engineering time, a
profile typical of research-intensive software projects in which
labour outweighs material amortisation, so reductions in scope or
schedule translate directly into reductions in cost.
The interpretation relevant to SDG~9 is that the project's economic
footprint is not material-intensive: no new physical infrastructure
is built, and the budget is mostly a transfer of human capital.

\textbf{Useful life and state of the art.}
The alternatives for lead-sheet digitisation are manual transcription
and general-purpose OMR tools, both of which fall short on jazz
engraving for the reasons surveyed in \Cref{sec:soa-classical}.
A domain-specific CRNN--CTC model reduces the correction workload on
the target class of input and eliminates the per-seat licence costs of
commercial alternatives.

\textbf{Improvement contribution.}
The principal economic improvement is not zero-cost digitisation
but a \textit{lower cost per corrected page} after deployment.
A user of the system still has to verify and correct the output
manually for any application that depends on note-perfect
transcription, but the per-page correction time is reduced
because the model handles the bulk of the symbol recognition
correctly (\Cref{ch:results}).
A second improvement is research reproducibility: the open-source
release of the code, the training scripts, and the trained model
weights lowers the entry cost for future OMR research targeting
the same or adjacent domains, because the synthetic data pipeline
and the CRNN--CTC training stack do not have to be rebuilt from
scratch.

\subsection{Environmental dimension}
\label{sec:sust-environmental}
This dimension is the one most directly aligned with SDG~12
(Responsible Consumption and Production) and is treated quantitatively.

\textbf{PPP (design and development).}
The project's estimated energy consumption is approximately
\SI{62.5}{\kilo\watt\hour}, derived from a coarse but reproducible
multiplication of the rated power draw of the development
workstation by the cumulative GPU-engaged time over the twenty-one
week schedule.
The workstation pairs an NVIDIA RTX~3060 GPU (\SI{170}{\watt} TDP)
with a desktop platform whose full-system draw under training load
is approximately \SI{250}{\watt}.
Cumulative GPU-engaged time totals approximately
\SI{250}{\hour}: a single 90-epoch CRNN training takes about
\SI{13}{\hour}, and the figure aggregates the many re-trainings,
ablation runs, and chord-recognition fine-tuning carried out over the project,
with smaller contributions from synthetic-data rendering and
evaluation passes.
The energy estimate follows directly:
$\SI{250}{\hour} \times \SI{0.25}{\kilo\watt} \approx
\SI{62.5}{\kilo\watt\hour}$~\cite{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019,pattersonCarbonConsiderationsDeep2021,lacosteQuantifyingCarbonEmissions2019}.
Applying the Spanish electricity emission factor of approximately
\SI{0.23}{\kilo\gram\per\kilo\watt\hour} of CO\textsubscript{2}
reported by Red El\'ectrica de Espa\~na and
MITECO~\cite{reeInformeSistemaElectrico2024,mitecoFactorEmision2025}
yields roughly \SI{14.4}{\kilo\gram} of CO\textsubscript{2}e
emitted during the project's execution.

The dominant contributor to the total is GPU training time.
Inference, dataset generation, and development sessions away from
the GPU contribute much less per hour because they do not engage
the high-power-draw components of the workstation.
This concentration of environmental impact in a single activity
makes it tractable: optimising training reduces the total far more
than optimising the rest of the workflow.

\textbf{Minimisation plan.}
Four measures together keep the training-time energy within the
estimated envelope.
The architecture choice (\Cref{sec:design-model}) favours the
lightweight ResNet18 backbone over heavier alternatives that would add
only marginal accuracy on monophonic input, a trade-off made explicit
in \Cref{sec:mgmt-justification}.
Mixed-precision training via PyTorch's \code{torch.amp} cuts both VRAM
use and per-step time, which translates into fewer GPU-hours per
converged model.
Early stopping with a twelve-epoch patience halts training once the
validation SER stops improving, avoiding the common habit of running
well past convergence ``to be safe'' (on the final run the best SER
was reached near the end, at epoch 83 of 90, so the run reached the
cap without the patience triggering).
Finally, the project reuses existing hardware rather than provisioning
new equipment, whose embodied manufacturing energy would substantially
exceed the project's modest operational energy.

Data generation and training are also organised in batches to
avoid redundant runs: the dataset is fixed before model training
begins, so that data-pipeline iteration and model iteration do
not compound in cost.
Each significant training run is logged and its CSV summary
retained, which makes it possible to identify and avoid
duplicate experiments in later sprints.

\textbf{Useful life and improvement contribution.}
Once trained, inference has a low marginal energy cost compared
with retraining: a single page transcription completes in
seconds on consumer hardware and consumes a small fraction of a
watt-hour of energy.
The energy break-even point of the project, i.e.\ the number of
inference invocations at which the operational energy of inference
equals the one-time training cost, is therefore high enough that
the trained model can be regarded as an amortised asset.
Sharing the trained weights and the training scripts spreads the
one-time training cost across multiple downstream users and
multiple academic iterations, so that the per-application share
of the project's environmental footprint shrinks as the model is
re-used.

\textbf{Embodied and end-of-life impact.}
The operational figure above excludes two lifecycle stages that are
harder to quantify: the embodied energy of manufacturing the GPU and
workstation, paid once at fabrication and amortised across every run
on the reused hardware, and end-of-life disposal of the GPU through
the domestic e-waste channel.\todo{cite: figure for embodied carbon of a consumer GPU or workstation if a defensible source can be located (e.g.\ NVIDIA or Dell product carbon-footprint disclosures); otherwise leave as qualitative.}

\subsection{Social dimension}
\label{sec:sust-social}
The social dimension touches SDG~4 (Quality Education) through
improved access to editable teaching materials, and SDG~10
(Reduced Inequalities) through the accessibility argument developed
below.

\textbf{PPP (design and development).}
In personal-development terms the project strengthens competencies
in three areas relevant to the author's future professional work:
machine-learning engineering (architecture design, training-loop
implementation, evaluation methodology), project planning
(work-breakdown structures, sprint cadence, budget management), and
scientific communication (technical writing, figure-driven argument,
and the citation discipline required by a thesis-length document).
The project also reinforces ethical awareness about the limitations
and failure modes of automated transcription: working through the
residual-error analysis of \Cref{ch:results} produces a more
concrete picture of what the model gets right and wrong than a
single aggregate metric can convey.

\textbf{Useful life and beneficiaries.}
There is real demand for digitising lead sheets used in music
education and performance, and the system targets a notation style
(Real Book engraving) that is widely used and poorly served by
existing tools.
For \textit{musicians}, the downstream applications include
automatic transposition to a different key (essential for B$\flat$,
E$\flat$, and F transposing instruments), MIDI playback for
practice and accompaniment, and integration with practice apps that
depend on a symbolic representation of the score.
For \textit{teachers}, the value is in producing digital materials
that can be edited, transposed, and annotated for specific
pedagogical purposes without re-typesetting the entire piece by
hand.
For \textit{archivists}, OMR is a precondition for making large
paper collections searchable: a scanned PDF is preserved but not
discoverable, whereas a symbolic representation can be queried
melodically, harmonically, or rhythmically.
For \textit{researchers}, especially in music information retrieval
and computational musicology, a symbolic version of a large jazz
corpus enables statistical and structural analyses that cannot be
performed on raster images.

A related accessibility argument applies to \emph{sight-impaired
musicians}: the structured JSON output is in principle consumable
by screen readers and braille-music tooling, whereas a scanned PDF
is not, though realising this benefit depends on downstream software
the project does not itself ship.
Across the served groups, the project may reduce the repetitive
manual work of converting paper notation to a machine-readable form
and support the long-term preservation of music content that
currently exists only as paper or low-resolution scanned PDF.

The narrow domain specification leaves several groups outside the
system's contract, and the social value of the tool depends on
communicating these limits clearly. The monophonic-only filter
(\Cref{sec:design-data}) excludes polyphonic notation such as piano
scores, choral parts, and ensemble full scores; the treble-clef-only
assumption excludes F and C clefs; and the synthetic LilyJAZZ
training corpus targets the Real Book engraving style, so accuracy
on classical, historical, handwritten, or non-Western sources is not
the figure reported in \Cref{ch:results}. The residual error rate of
\Cref{ch:results}, while small in aggregate, is not zero, so
applications needing note-perfect transcription must include a human
verification step. Stating these boundaries keeps the system from
being deployed as a general-purpose music recogniser by accident.

\section{Commitment and Ethical Position}
\label{sec:sust-commitment}

The project's ethical position aligns with the open-science
commitments of the GEP sustainability framework, and is recorded
here as five concrete practices rather than a single statement of
principle.

\textit{Open source release.}
The source code is released under a permissive open-source
licence and is tracked in a public Git repository
(\Cref{app:code}).
Every experiment in the thesis can be reproduced from a clean
clone using a pinned-dependency environment managed by Poetry
(\Cref{sec:impl-reproducibility}), and the trained model
weights are distributed alongside the code.
This commitment matters because OMR systems often disappear into
private repositories that cannot be inspected or extended; making
the implementation public is the most direct contribution the
project can make to the surrounding research community.

\textit{Documented evaluation protocol.}
The metric protocol used in \Cref{ch:results} is documented
in full, including the definition of the aggregate Symbol Error
Rate, the melodic SER that strips structural tokens, and the
category-level error breakdown that decomposes edits into
substitutions, insertions, and deletions per token class.
Future work can therefore replicate the present evaluation
exactly, or extend it with additional metrics, without having to
guess at how the present numbers were computed.

\textit{Licence-respecting use of third-party data.}
All third-party data used in the project is consumed in
accordance with its licence.
PrIMuS, the synthetic-supervision source, is distributed by the Pattern
Recognition and Artificial Intelligence Group (PRAIG) at the University of
Alicante for non-commercial research use~\cite{PrIMuSDataset} and is used
here unmodified.
The training corpus visible to the CRNN is entirely synthetic:
LilyJAZZ renders generated from PrIMuS \texttt{.semantic} files, so
no copyrighted Real Book page is part of the music CRNN's
training-time inputs.
The chord branch is trained primarily on synthetic LilyJAZZ chord
strips, with fine-tuning on a small set of hand-labelled strips
extracted by \code{extract\_real\_chord\_strips} from privately held
Real Book scans.
The Real Book itself is a copyrighted commercial publication (Hal
Leonard), and any private scans used by the author are restricted
to evaluation and qualitative inspection in a strictly academic
context; they are never redistributed, and the released model
weights do not embed recoverable copies of those pages: both pipelines
learn symbolic token sequences, not pixels.
This use falls within the academic exceptions granted by Spanish
intellectual-property law (\textit{Real Decreto Legislativo
1/1996}, art.~32, on quotation and illustration for teaching and
scientific research~\cite{spainLPI1996}) and by the text-and-data-mining
exception for research organisations established in art.~3 of the EU
Digital Single Market Directive (2019/790)~\cite{euDSMDirective2019}.

\textit{No personal data, no safety-critical decisions.}
The system does not process personal data at any stage: inputs are
music notation, outputs are symbolic representations of that
notation, and it is not deployed for safety-critical automated
decision-making.
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR,
Regulation (EU) 2016/679~\cite{euGDPR2016}) is therefore not engaged
by the project's training, inference, or deployment surface, since
no identifiable natural persons are referenced in any data stream.
This narrows the ethical surface of the project to its
intellectual-property dimension and to its honesty about its
capabilities.

\textit{Misuse considerations.}
The open-source release also lowers the barrier for foreseeable
misuse: at-scale digitisation and redistribution of copyrighted lead
sheets, circumvention of paid score libraries, or unlicensed
derivative works generated from the symbolic output.
The released artefacts are limited to code, training scripts, and
weights trained exclusively on synthetic PrIMuS-derived data, with
no Real-Book-derived corpus or pre-extracted symbol library, and the
capabilities and limitations are documented in this thesis and in
the public \code{README}.

\textit{Documented limitations.}
The known limitations of the system---the monophonic contract,
the treble-clef-only assumption, the eight allowed time
signatures, and the residual SER profile---are documented
prominently in the project's \code{README}, in the project
overview documentation, and in the present thesis
(\Cref{sec:scope} and \Cref{sec:sust-social}).
Future users of the model can therefore evaluate in advance
whether the system is suitable for their use case rather than
discovering its limitations through failure in production.

\section{Final Reflection}
\label{sec:sust-final}

Revisiting the sustainability assessment at the end of the project,
two updates are worth recording.

First, the \textbf{environmental} estimate of $\approx 62.5$\,kWh
is consistent with the executed training profile: the best checkpoint
was reached at epoch \num{83} of a 90-epoch run (\Cref{sec:res-training})
and the full training run including ablations fits within the planned
envelope.
Mixed-precision training was the single most effective mitigation:
it reduced VRAM usage enough that the entire pipeline runs on a
consumer GPU, avoiding the cloud-rental scenario reserved as the
worst-case fallback in the incidentals table
(\Cref{tab:incidentals}).

Second, the \textbf{social} value of the system depends on the
deployment surface, not just on its accuracy in isolation.
The FastAPI web service of \Cref{sec:impl-api} packages the
inference pipeline behind a single REST endpoint with no GPU
requirement on the client side, which is the deployment shape
intended to make the tool accessible to musicians, educators, and
archivists who do not have local ML expertise.
Realising that accessibility in practice requires a hosted
deployment that is outside the scope of the present thesis; what
the thesis demonstrates is that the technical preconditions for a
low-friction deployment are met.
This shape was a planned design decision, but its sustainability
significance only becomes visible once the technical work is
complete: without an accessible deployment surface, the same accuracy
would reach far fewer people.

A residual concern is the \textbf{lifecycle} of the trained model
weights, which become obsolete as new architectures are released,
as datasets evolve, and as the target notation style drifts.
Publishing the training scripts alongside the weights lets the cost
of training a replacement be paid once and redistributed, rather
than repeated independently by each successor project.
