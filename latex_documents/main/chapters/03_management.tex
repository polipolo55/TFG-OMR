\chapter{Project Management (GEP)}
\label{ch:mgmt}

This chapter records the management dimension of the project: the
comparative justification of the chosen architecture against the
plausible alternatives, the iterative methodology adopted to execute
it, the work-breakdown structure and Gantt schedule, the risk register
and contingency plans, and the economic budget with its control
mechanism.
The introductory framing of the project---academic context, scope,
objectives, and stakeholders---is given in Chapter~\ref{ch:intro}
and is not repeated here.

\section{Justification of the Chosen Architecture}
\label{sec:mgmt-justification}

\subsection{Existing alternatives}

Section~\ref{sec:soa-classical}, Section~\ref{sec:soa-crnn}, and
Section~\ref{sec:soa-transformer} surveyed the three principal
families of OMR systems in the literature.
Considered as candidate architectures for this thesis, they are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Traditional modular pipelines} (staff
          detection/removal, symbol segmentation, symbol
          classification, reconstruction) with the well-known error
          propagation issues catalogued by Dalitz et
          al.~\cite{dalitzComparativeStudyStaff2008} and the modern
          survey~\cite{calvoZaragozaUnderstandingOMR2020}.
    \item \textbf{CRNN--CTC sequence models}, well-suited to
          left-to-right staff-line transcription and the de facto
          baseline for monophonic
          OMR~\cite{calvoZaragozaEndToEndNeural2018,PrIMuSDataset}.
    \item \textbf{Transformer-based end-to-end models}, stronger for
          full-page and polyphonic
          settings~\cite{riosVilaSheetMusicTransformer2024,riosVilaSMTPlusPlus2024,yangLEGATO2025}
          but with substantially heavier data and compute
          requirements.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Selection criteria}

The decision is based on six criteria reflecting both the technical
target and the practical constraints of a Bachelor's thesis.

\textit{Expected accuracy on monophonic lines.}
The principal deliverable of the project is a measurable Symbol
Error Rate on monophonic jazz lead sheets, so any architecture
whose published accuracy on monophonic input is substantially
below the state of the art is disqualified at the outset.

\textit{Computational feasibility on consumer hardware.}
Training must complete within a few days on a single NVIDIA
RTX~3060 with 12\,GB of VRAM, the hardware available to the
author.
Any model whose memory or compute footprint exceeds that envelope
either has to be downscaled---defeating its accuracy
advantage---or trained in cloud rentals, which is incompatible
with the project budget.

\textit{Data requirements.}
The only labelled corpus directly usable for monophonic OMR is
PrIMuS~\cite{PrIMuSDataset}, with approximately 88\,000 incipits;
the closest jazz-specific corpus emerged only in mid-2025 and is
substantially
smaller~\cite{martinezSevillaJazzLeadSheets2025}.
Architectures whose published results rely on hundreds of
thousands of polyphonic samples are infeasible without a
data-acquisition phase that exceeds the scope of this thesis.

\textit{Implementation complexity.}
A single-person project does not have the engineering bandwidth to
debug a custom transformer training stack in parallel with a
synthetic data pipeline.
A well-documented architecture with mature open-source
reference implementations is preferred.

\textit{Reproducibility within the project's timeline.}
Each sprint cycle must terminate with a measurable, reproducible
result.
Architectures whose training is highly stochastic, or whose
hyperparameter space is large enough to require dozens of trial
runs before convergence, would consume the project's iteration
budget before producing a stable baseline.

\textit{Extension potential for future work.}
The thesis records the design space available to follow-up
work; an architecture that has natural extensions to polyphony,
full-page input, or chord-symbol integration is preferred over
one that locks the line of research into a single configuration.

Table~\ref{tab:alt-comparison} shows that CRNN--CTC is the best fit
for this stage: it provides enough modelling capacity for monophonic
lead sheets while remaining trainable and debuggable within the
available timeframe and hardware budget.
A transformer would in principle yield higher accuracy on the
monophonic line, but the data and compute costs of a from-scratch
training run are incompatible with the resources of a single-person
project.
A traditional modular pipeline is cheap to implement but is
penalised by error propagation across stages and by its sensitivity
to the jazz engraving style; this option is in fact retained as the
\textit{lower bound} baseline against which the CRNN--CTC results
are compared in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Chosen alternative}

The selected architecture is a \textbf{two-stream CRNN--CTC system}
in which a music CRNN--CTC transcribes the monophonic staff line and
a second, independently trained CRNN--CTC performs OCR on the chord
strip above the staff; its design is the subject of
Chapter~\ref{ch:design} and its implementation of
Chapter~\ref{ch:impl}.
The decision balances modelling capacity against feasibility: the
model is lighter than transformer alternatives, has strong prior
art in OMR datasets, and supports robust sequence prediction
without requiring per-symbol segmentation of the input image.
Splitting the chord recogniser into a separate network keeps both
vocabularies small and lets each branch be trained and debugged
independently.

Three concrete consequences follow from this choice.
First, the training corpus can be built entirely from a single
public dataset (PrIMuS) re-rendered in the target engraving style,
which keeps the data-acquisition cost within the budget allocated
for the thesis.
Second, the output representation can be a compact flat token
sequence (LMX, Section~\ref{sec:lmx-format}) consumed by both the
music and the chord branches, avoiding the verbose-XML output that
transformer-decoder architectures typically produce.
Third, the model's training time on a consumer GPU is short enough
(of the order of a day per full run) that several training
iterations per sprint are realistic; this is what makes the
iterative methodology of Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-methodology}
workable in practice.

The decision is also a risk-control choice.
A traditional modular pipeline (the lower-bound baseline) can be
implemented early and serves as a reference point for measuring
the CRNN--CTC's contribution; the CRNN--CTC model is then
iteratively improved through data and training refinements.
This staged execution path still yields publishable results even
if the more ambitious extensions---fine-tuning on real Real Book
pages, chord OCR, full-page inference---cannot all be completed
within the available time.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:mgmt-methodology}

The project follows an \textbf{iterative and incremental}
methodology with sprint-based checkpoints and supervisor reviews.
Each cycle is required to produce measurable
artefacts---datasets, training logs, SER values, error
analyses---rather than internal milestones whose progress cannot
be inspected from the outside.
This produces an audit trail that grounds every replanning
decision in evidence rather than in subjective progress
estimates, and gives the supervisor concrete material to react
to at each biweekly meeting.

Execution is explicitly organised as a closed loop between three
activities: \textit{data construction} (rendering, augmentation,
vocabulary), \textit{model training} (architecture changes,
hyperparameter sweeps, training-curve monitoring), and
\textit{evaluation and error analysis} (SER measurement,
per-token breakdown, qualitative case studies).
Each of the three is revisited multiple times during the
project: an error analysis that identifies a systematic failure
mode triggers a data-pipeline change, which triggers a new
training run, whose results feed back into the next error
analysis.
A naïve waterfall reading of the work-package structure
(WP1 $\to$ WP2 $\to$ WP3 $\to$ WP4 $\to$ WP5) would mistake the
\textit{first} traversal of this loop for the \textit{only}
traversal; in practice, sprint reviews regularly send the
project back to a prior WP for incremental refinement, and the
budget reserves time for that.

Four practices enforce methodological rigour and are applied
uniformly across the project.

\textit{Traceability.}
Every milestone artefact---a dataset version, a model
checkpoint, an SER measurement---is linked to a specific
repository commit and to a documented design decision.
This makes it possible to reconstruct, several months after the
fact, exactly which version of the data and which version of the
code produced any given experimental number.
Without traceability, ablation studies and regression checks
become unreliable, because there is no fixed reference point
against which to compare.

\textit{Reproducibility.}
Data generation, training, and evaluation are all driven by
scripts rather than by interactive notebook sessions, and the
full \code{Config} dataclass is embedded in every checkpoint
(Section~\ref{sec:impl-reproducibility}).
A consequence is that any experiment reported in
Chapter~\ref{ch:results} can be re-run with a single CLI
invocation from a clean clone of the repository on a different
machine; this is enforced as Hard Constraint~3 in the project's
contributor guide.

\textit{Quantitative control.}
Symbol Error Rate is measured at every significant training
iteration, not only at the end of a sprint.
Side metrics---training/validation loss gap, per-token
edit-distance breakdown, per-sample SER distribution---are
computed alongside, so that a single aggregate number is never
the basis for a design decision.
The category-level error breakdown of
Section~\ref{sec:res-errors} is the direct product of this
practice.

\textit{Corrective loop.}
Each sprint ends with a review that can adjust scope, effort
estimates, or risk responses for the next sprint.
The review is held with the supervisor and consumes a fixed
budget (three hours per sprint, accounted for in
Table~\ref{tab:task-summary}); it produces a short written note
on whether the sprint hit its objectives, what evidence supports
that judgement, and what is carried forward into the next
sprint.
The note is the input to the budget-control mechanism described
in Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-control}.

Monitoring tools include Git and GitHub for version control,
per-experiment training logs and CSV summaries written into the
model directory, and biweekly milestone meetings with the
supervisor at which the artefacts produced in the previous
sprint are reviewed and the priorities for the next one are
agreed.

\section{Project Planning}
\label{sec:mgmt-planning}

\subsection{Work-breakdown structure}

The plan spans 21 weeks (February--June 2026), organised into five
sprint-aligned work packages (WP1--WP5) plus a transversal
management work package (WP0) that runs in parallel throughout the
project.
Execution control uses \textbf{fixed four-week sprints} aligned
with the work packages (each WP1--WP4 is one sprint; WP5 closes
the project over five weeks), with a biweekly supervisor check-in
inside each sprint.
Backlog priorities, risks, and effort estimates are reviewed at
every sprint boundary and may be revised in either direction.

The six work packages have the following internal logic.

\textit{WP0 -- Project management (130\,h, concurrent).}
This package contains the activities that span the project rather
than belonging to any one technical phase: the writing of the GEP
deliverables, the biweekly supervisor meetings, and the
continuous writing of the thesis document.
It is intentionally allocated a substantial budget (\SI{26}{\percent}
of total effort) because the GEP coursework requires four
deliverables in its own right and because deferring thesis
writing until the final weeks is one of the principal failure
modes of single-person projects.

\textit{WP1 -- Literature review and baseline (78\,h).}
This package establishes the analytical foundation: a literature
survey of OMR (the material that becomes Chapter~\ref{ch:soa}),
an exploration of the public OMR datasets that informs the
data-strategy choice, and a classical computer-vision baseline
that provides a quantitative reference against which the
CRNN--CTC results can be compared.
The classical baseline also serves a risk-control function: if
the CRNN--CTC model fails to converge, the baseline ensures the
project still has measurable results to report.

\textit{WP2 -- Dataset construction (73\,h).}
This package builds the synthetic training corpus that the
CRNN--CTC requires.
The output of WP2 is the LMX vocabulary, the LilyJAZZ rendering
pipeline, the scan-simulation augmentation pipeline, and the
train/validation/test splits.
WP2 must complete before WP3 can begin in earnest, because the
model architecture is dimensioned to the vocabulary the dataset
produces.

\textit{WP3 -- CRNN--CTC development (75\,h).}
This package designs and implements the model, the training loop,
and the evaluation framework.
The training-pipeline task (T3.2) overlaps deliberately with the
evaluation-framework task (T3.4) because the latter can be
developed against synthetic CRNN outputs before the former has
fully converged.
The package ends with an initial end-to-end training run whose
outputs feed directly into WP4.

\textit{WP4 -- Iterative refinement (68\,h).}
This package closes the train--evaluate--improve loop: error
analysis identifies systematic failure modes, the data pipeline
and the training configuration are adjusted, and the model is
retrained.
This is the work package where most of the closed-loop behaviour
described in Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-methodology} actually
happens; its 68-hour budget is split across roughly two sprints
of refinement and ablations.

\textit{WP5 -- Final evaluation and defence (85\,h).}
This package contains the final evaluation on held-out data, the
qualitative error analysis, a conditional extension task (T5.3,
budgeted at 20\,h and contingent on the monophonic objective
having been achieved), the final integration pass on the thesis
document, and the defence preparation.
The conditional extension task is the most important schedule
buffer in the plan: it can be reduced to zero without
compromising the core deliverable.

\subsection{Tasks, dependencies, and resources}

Table~\ref{tab:task-summary} lists the 31 tasks of the plan with
their effort estimates, dependencies, and principal resources.
The estimates are expressed in hours per task and were validated
against the author's available weekly workload of four to five
effective working hours per day, which corresponds to
approximately 24\,h per week of sustained project effort over the
21-week schedule.

The dependency column distinguishes between strict
dependencies---a task cannot begin until its predecessor is
complete---and partial dependencies, marked ``\textit{partial}'',
where a task may begin once its predecessor has reached a
sufficient maturity to provide the required input.
Partial dependencies are used sparingly, since they are
difficult to coordinate in a single-person project where context
switching has a real cost.

Concurrency in the schedule is intentionally limited.
A single-person project does not benefit from the parallelism
that drives multi-person planning, because the same individual
cannot productively split attention across more than one major
task in the same sprint.
Overlap is therefore introduced only when two tasks are
naturally compatible: for example, developing the evaluation
framework (T3.4) while the first training run (T3.3) is in
progress, since training runs in the background on the GPU and
the developer is otherwise idle.

\subsection{Gantt chart}

Figure~\ref{fig:gantt} shows the full 21-week timeline.
The chart is grouped by work package for readability; planning
control is performed at the sprint level with a fixed four-week
cadence and an inspection/adaptation checkpoint at every
boundary.
Sprint reviews are marked as red diamonds (M1 through M4),
together with the final defence at week 21 (M5).

Three structural features of the chart deserve specific
attention.
First, the management package (WP0) spans the entire timeline,
reflecting the policy of continuous thesis writing and biweekly
supervisor meetings; the four GEP-specific tasks (T0.1--T0.4)
are concentrated in the first half of the project to align with
the GEP coursework deadlines.
Second, the technical packages WP1--WP4 are scheduled
back-to-back with no slack between them, because each one
depends on the deliverable of its predecessor in a way that does
not admit overlap.
The schedule resilience instead comes from the conditional
extension task in WP5, which can be reduced if any of the
earlier packages overruns its budget.
Third, the bars within each sprint deliberately do not all
extend to the sprint boundary; this leaves room within each
sprint for the corrective-loop activities of
Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-methodology}, which would otherwise have
to come out of the next sprint's allocation.

\section{Risk Register and Contingency Plans}
\label{sec:mgmt-risks}

The risk register identifies six principal risks that were
foreseeable at the start of the project and for which explicit
mitigations or alternative plans can be prepared in advance.
Each risk is rated on two axes---likelihood and
impact---using a three-level scale (low, medium, high), and is
paired with an alternative plan, an estimate of how much
schedule the activation of that plan would consume, and an
estimate of any additional resources it would require.
The register is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:risk-register}; the
discussion below explains the reasoning behind each entry.

\textit{Limited ground-truth data on real Real Book pages
(likelihood high, impact high).}
The fine-tuning stage of the training plan requires manually
labelled scans of real Real Book pages, which the author must
produce.
If labelling proves slower than estimated---an outcome made more
likely by the absence of a similar corpus to bootstrap
from---the project falls back to the synthetic
LilyJAZZ-augmented corpus alone, accepting that the
synthetic-to-real domain gap will remain partly open.
The fallback consumes up to ten hours of reallocated effort but
requires no additional resources, because the synthetic
pipeline is already implemented.

\textit{Staff-detection errors propagating downstream
(likelihood medium, impact medium).}
The morphological staff detector can fail on degraded scans, and
its failures propagate irreversibly into the CRNN's input.
The fallback is to use manually cropped staff regions for
critical evaluations, sidestepping the detector entirely for the
purposes of measuring CRNN-only accuracy.
This shifts hours from the conditional extension task (T5.3) to
the final thesis-writing task (T5.4) but does not require new
resources.

\textit{Encoding-design complexity (likelihood medium, impact medium).}
The LMX vocabulary is the only output representation the model
can produce; if the initial vocabulary design proves too rich
(too many rare classes that the model under-fits) or too
restrictive (missing tokens that the dataset emits), training
results will be unreliable until the vocabulary is revised.
The fallback is to start from a minimal vocabulary and extend it
incrementally after the model has reached a stable convergence
profile.
This consumes up to six hours of redesign within the project
buffer and does not propagate further.

\textit{Model underfitting or CTC misalignment
(likelihood medium, impact high).}
CTC training is known to fail catastrophically if the input time
budget is shorter than the target token sequence, or if the
model's capacity is insufficient for the vocabulary.
The fallback is to simplify the vocabulary, retrain on cleaned
data subsets, and adjust the image-scale parameter so that the
time axis available to the CTC alignment is sufficient.
This consumes up to ten hours from the project buffer and
requires additional GPU time, accounted for in the incidentals
table.

\textit{Scope creep toward polyphony (likelihood medium, impact high).}
A persistent risk in OMR research is the temptation to extend
the model to polyphonic input before the monophonic objective
has been achieved.
The mitigation is structural rather than reactive: the project
scope (Section~\ref{sec:scope}) explicitly excludes polyphony,
and the conditional extension task (T5.3) is restricted to a
feasibility discussion rather than implementation.
If the temptation arises mid-project, up to eighteen hours of
T5.3's budget can be recovered for the core monophonic
deliverable.

\textit{Computational resource constraints
(likelihood low-to-medium, impact medium).}
The available hardware (an NVIDIA RTX~3060 with 12\,GB of VRAM)
fits the baseline model comfortably but provides limited
headroom for hyperparameter sweeps.
If concurrent obligations occupy the workstation or if a
significant resize of the model becomes necessary, the fallback
is a short cloud-GPU rental (a single AWS or Lambda Labs
session, of the order of \EUR{}20--50) that lets the project
clear the bottleneck without impact on the core schedule.
Setup consumes up to five hours and is the only entry in the
register that requires direct procurement.

\bigskip

To improve schedule robustness, the plan includes explicit slack
in three places:
conservative estimation in the two highest-uncertainty tasks
(dataset rendering and model refinement);
the conditional extension task T5.3, which can be reduced
without compromising the core objective;
and progressive thesis writing throughout execution rather than
the more common pattern of postponing all writing to the final
weeks.
Together, these three buffers absorb the schedule consequences
of the lower-impact risks without triggering the
budget-control mechanism described in
Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-control}.

\section{Economic Management}
\label{sec:mgmt-budget}

\subsection{Budget structure}

The budget follows the standard software-project cost framework
taught in the FIB GEP module~\cite{gepModule24EconomicManagement}
and is organised into four components: \textit{human resources by
activity}, itemised against the task structure of
Section~\ref{sec:mgmt-planning}; \textit{general costs}, covering
hardware amortisation, electricity and connectivity overheads, and
software-licence costs (zero, since the entire toolchain is open
source); \textit{incidentals}, which translate each risk's
contingency action into an expected-value contribution (full cost
$\times$ likelihood); and a \textit{contingency reserve} of fifteen
per cent of the baseline (human resources plus general costs) for
the residual uncertainty that the risk register cannot enumerate
explicitly.
The wider sustainability framing of this budget---useful life,
break-even, and the economic-impact assessment proper---is
deferred to Section~\ref{sec:sust-economic}, which uses the
totals derived here as its inputs.

\subsection{Human resources}

Hourly rates are derived from the Spanish ICT salary survey
INE-EAES~\cite{ineEncuestaEstructuraSalarial2023} and loaded with
a \num{1.35} multiplier that captures the employer's
social-security contributions in the General Regime of the
Spanish social-security
system~\cite{segSocialCotizacion2025}.
This multiplier converts the gross annual salary used as a
public reference number into the fully loaded annual cost that
an employer actually incurs, which is the figure relevant for
project budgeting.
The hourly rate is then derived from the loaded annual cost
assuming a working year of 1\,760 hours
(220 working days, 8 hours per day, after holidays), as prescribed
by the FIB GEP economic-management

\subsection{Budget control}
\label{sec:mgmt-control}

Budget tracking is performed at each sprint review using three
indicators drawn from the earned-value-management
framework~\cite{pmiBOKGuide2021}.
The framework requires three quantities per sprint: the
\textit{budgeted cost of work scheduled} (BCWS), which is the
planned cost of the tasks the sprint should have completed;
the \textit{budgeted cost of work performed} (BCWP), which is
the planned cost of the tasks actually completed; and the
\textit{actual cost of work performed} (ACWP), which is the
real cost incurred to complete those tasks.
From these three primitives the indicators are derived as
follows.

\textit{Cost variance.}
$\mathrm{CV} = \mathrm{BCWP} - \mathrm{ACWP}$ is the difference
between the budgeted cost of completed work and what it actually
cost.
A positive CV means the sprint came in under budget; a negative
CV means it overran.
This is the headline cost-health indicator for a single sprint.

\textit{Schedule variance.}
$\mathrm{SV} = \mathrm{BCWP} - \mathrm{BCWS}$ is the difference
between the work completed and the work planned, both measured in
the common currency of budgeted cost.
A positive SV means the sprint completed more than was planned; a
negative SV means it fell behind.
SV is reported alongside CV because cost and schedule can move in
opposite directions---a sprint can come in under cost simply by
not completing all its work, which CV alone would not catch.

\textit{Cost performance index.}
$\mathrm{CPI} = \mathrm{BCWP} / \mathrm{ACWP}$ is the
dimensionless ratio of value earned to cost incurred.
A CPI of 1.0 indicates exact agreement with the plan, below 1.0
indicates an overrun, and above 1.0 indicates an underrun.
The advantage of the index form over the raw variance is that it
is directly comparable across sprints of different sizes.

\textit{Trigger thresholds.}
Corrective actions are triggered when at least one of the
following conditions holds in a sprint review:
$|\mathrm{CV}| > \SI{10}{\percent}$ of the sprint budget,
$\mathrm{SV} < -\SI{5}{\percent}$ of the sprint budget, or
$\mathrm{CPI} < 0.90$ for two consecutive reviews.
A single low CPI reading is not by itself a trigger because
single sprints have substantial natural variance in a
single-person project; sustained underperformance over two
sprints is required before the corrective mechanism activates.
Once triggered, the corrective action is recorded in the sprint
review note and may activate one of the entries of the risk
register (Table~\ref{tab:risk-register})---typically the
reallocation of effort from the conditional extension task
(T5.3) to the affected work package, or the acceptance of the
short cloud-GPU rental from the incidentals budget.
